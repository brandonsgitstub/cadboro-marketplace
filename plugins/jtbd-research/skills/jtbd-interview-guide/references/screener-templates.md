# Screener Templates

Screeners for JTBD interviews have one goal: find people who have recently made a meaningful decision in the relevant space. Steady-state users don't remember why they made choices. Recent decision-makers do.

---

## Core screener principles

1. **Target the recent decision, not the user type.** Don't screen for "people who use CRM software." Screen for "people who evaluated or switched CRM software in the last 6 months."

2. **Set a recency window.** 3–6 months is ideal for most SaaS decisions. Beyond 6 months, memory degrades and emotional context fades. For high-stakes or infrequent decisions (e.g. enterprise procurement), extend to 12 months.

3. **Screen for diversity of outcome.** Don't only recruit people who chose your product or the competitor you're worried about. Include people who chose differently, chose nothing, or went back to a previous approach. They often have the most revealing data.

4. **Keep screeners short.** 4–6 questions maximum. The goal is qualification, not research. Long screeners reduce completion rates and don't add signal.

5. **Always include a disqualifier.** One question should actively filter out people who don't meet the core criterion. This protects interview quality.

---

## Screener by research context

### Context A: Understanding why users adopt a new tool or switch

Use when: exploring activation problems, competitive positioning, new market entry

```
Screener questions:

1. In the last [3–6] months, have you evaluated, tried, or switched to a new [product category — e.g. project management tool, CRM, analytics platform] for your work?
   → YES: continue | NO: disqualify

2. Did you ultimately adopt a new tool, or did you decide to stick with what you had?
   → Record answer — recruit both outcomes for diversity

3. Were you the primary decision-maker in this evaluation, or was someone else leading it?
   → Primary or heavily involved: continue | Not involved: disqualify

4. Roughly how many people on your team are affected by this tool?
   → Record for segment diversity

5. What industry is your company in?
   → Use to control for segment if relevant to your research
```

### Context B: Understanding why users churn or disengage

Use when: retention is underperforming, users are leaving or going inactive

```
Screener questions:

1. In the last [3–6] months, have you stopped using or significantly reduced your use of [product or product category]?
   → YES: continue | NO: disqualify

2. Did you switch to a different solution, or did you stop using this type of tool altogether?
   → Record answer — both are valid; "stopped altogether" is often the most revealing

3. Were you the primary user of the tool, or did others on your team use it more?
   → Primary user: continue | Rarely used: disqualify

4. Before you stopped or reduced use, how long had you been using it?
   → Record — users who churned early vs. late have different jobs

5. What was your main reason for reducing use? (open-ended — do not provide options)
   → Use responses to assess fit for your research question; don't disqualify based on answer
```

### Context C: Understanding an existing user segment's jobs

Use when: roadmap planning, expansion, or exploring adjacent opportunities within current user base

```
Screener questions:

1. How long have you been using [product or product category] in your current role?
   → Less than 1 month: disqualify (too new, insufficient context) | 1 month or more: continue

2. How frequently do you use it?
   → At least weekly: continue | Less than weekly: disqualify unless infrequent use is the specific subject of research

3. In the last [3–6] months, has anything significant changed about how you use it — or about what you're trying to accomplish with it?
   → YES: flag as high-priority recruit (change events produce rich JTBD data) | NO: still qualify but de-prioritize

4. What's your primary role?
   → Use to control for segment if you're researching a specific persona

5. What's the size of your company?
   → Use to control for company maturity if relevant
```

### Context D: Exploratory — new market or segment

Use when: evaluating whether to build for a new user type or enter a new market

```
Screener questions:

1. In your role, how often do you need to [high-level description of the job space — e.g. "report on team performance to senior stakeholders"]?
   → At least monthly: continue | Rarely or never: disqualify

2. What tools or approaches do you currently use to do this? (open-ended)
   → Record — reveals competitive landscape; no disqualifier

3. In the last [6–12] months, have you looked for a new or better way to handle this?
   → YES: flag as high-priority | NO: still qualify — people not actively looking often have the most important unmet needs

4. How many people are involved in this process with you?
   → Record for context

5. What does "good" look like when this goes well?
   → Screen out if the answer reveals a fundamentally different job than the one you're researching
```

## Screener delivery notes

- **Survey tools:** Typeform, Google Forms, or a simple email are all fine. Don't over-engineer the screener.
- **Incentives:** For 45–60 min interviews, $75–150 USD gift card is standard for individual contributors; $150–250 for directors and above.
- **Sample size for JTBD:** 6–10 interviews is typically sufficient to reach saturation on jobs and themes. Fewer than 5 produces directional data only. More than 12 in a single round is usually diminishing returns.
- **Time to field:** Allow 1–2 weeks for screener distribution and scheduling. Build this into research planning.
