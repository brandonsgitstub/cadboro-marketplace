# Question Sequences

Full question bank for JTBD interviews, organized by section. These are starting points — always adapt language to the specific research context before including in a guide.

Questions marked `[skip if short on time]` can be dropped without breaking the interview flow.
Questions marked `[pause — let them think]` benefit from silence after asking.

---

## Section 2 — Warm-up (5–10 min)

Purpose (for interviewer, not read aloud): Build rapport, establish shared context, and prime episodic memory by getting the participant talking about their actual work — not their job title or opinions.

**Q1 — Role and context**
"To start, can you tell me a bit about your role and what your team is responsible for?"

*Why:* Gets them talking in their own language. Job titles are often misleading. This surfaces how they actually describe their work.

**Q2 — Team and workflow context**
"What does a typical [day / week] look like for you when it comes to [relevant workflow area]?"

*Why:* Establishes the workflow context before asking about specific events. Helps the interviewer understand what "normal" looks like so deviations become meaningful.

**Q3 — Recent relevant activity** *(pick the one most relevant to your research)*
- "Can you walk me through the last time you had to [relevant task — e.g. 'put together a report for leadership']?"
- "Think back to the last time you dealt with [relevant problem area]. What was going on?"
- "What's been the biggest challenge in [relevant area] over the last few months?"

*Why:* Anchors the conversation to a specific recent event. Activates episodic memory rather than general opinions.

**Q4 — [skip if short on time] Tools and current approach**
"What tools or processes do you use today to handle [relevant area]?"

*Why:* Surfaces the competitive landscape and establishes what the participant considers "normal." Useful context before the hiring trigger sequence.

## Section 3 — Hiring Trigger Sequence (15–20 min)

Purpose (for interviewer, not read aloud): Find the specific moment this person decided to seek a new or better solution. Understand what changed, what they were feeling, and what was at stake.

**The anchor question — start here every time**
"I'd like to go back to the moment when you first realized you needed to [find a new tool / change how you were doing this / look for a better solution]. Can you take me back to that moment? What was going on?"

*Note to interviewer:* This is the most important question in the interview. Don't rush past it. If they give a generic answer ("we just needed something better"), probe: "What specifically triggered that feeling? Was there a moment or event that made it feel urgent?"

`[pause — let them think]`

**T1 — The triggering event**
"What specifically happened that made you think 'I need to do something different here'?"

*If they give a vague answer:*
"Was there a specific situation — maybe a meeting, a deadline, a piece of feedback — that crystallized it for you?"

**T2 — Timeline**
"When was this? How long had things been feeling that way before that moment?"

*Why:* Distinguishes between a sudden trigger (acute pain) and a slow-burning frustration that finally crossed a threshold. Both produce different job insights.

**T3 — Stakes and emotion**
"What was at stake for you personally at that point?"
"How were you feeling about it?"

*Why:* Surfaces the emotional job. This question often produces the most honest and revealing answers in the interview. Don't skip it.

`[pause — let them think]`

**T4 — [skip if short on time] Others involved**
"Was anyone else involved in recognizing the problem or deciding something needed to change?"
"How did they see the situation?"

*Why:* Surfaces the social job and reveals whether this was an individual or organizational decision. Critical for understanding B2B buying dynamics.

**T5 — The first step**
"What was the very first thing you did once you decided to look for something different?"

*Why:* Reveals how people begin their search. This is valuable for positioning and awareness channel insights, separate from the core JTBD data.

## Section 4 — Progress and Struggle Sequence (15–20 min)

Purpose (for interviewer, not read aloud): Map what the participant was trying to accomplish, where they got stuck, and what trade-offs they accepted. This section surfaces the functional job, the progress blockers, and the workarounds — the richest source of product opportunity.

**P1 — The job statement anchor**
"So when you were looking for a solution, what were you ultimately trying to be able to do? What would 'solved' have looked like?"

`[pause — let them think]`

*Why:* This is where the functional job statement comes from. Let them articulate it in their own words. Do not suggest answers.

**P2 — What they tried first**
"What did you try first? Walk me through what happened."

*Why:* First attempts reveal what the person assumed would solve the job — and why it didn't. This often exposes the gap between how PMs describe their product and how users understand it.

**P3 — Where things got hard**
"Where did you hit a wall or run into friction?"
"What was harder than you expected it to be?"

*Why:* The struggle map comes from this question. Probe for specifics: "Can you show me an example?" or "What did that look like in practice?"

**P4 — Workarounds**
"What did you do when you hit that point? How did you work around it?"

*Why:* Workarounds are goldmines. They reveal unmet needs disguised as user behaviour. A PM who sees "users export to Excel and manually merge the data" is looking at a product opportunity, not a user error.

**P5 — [skip if short on time] The trade-offs accepted**
"What did you give up or compromise on in the solution you ended up with?"
"What still doesn't work perfectly?"

*Why:* Even satisfied users have unmet needs. This question surfaces the residual jobs — the things the current solution doesn't address — which are often the best opportunity areas.

**P6 — The switch moment** *(only if they changed tools or approaches)*
"What finally made you commit to [new approach / tool]? What pushed you over the line?"

*Why:* The switch moment is where push + pull overcame anxiety + habit. Understanding it reveals what actually matters when someone decides to change — which is often different from what made them start looking.

**P7 — [skip if short on time] What they'd tell a colleague**
"If a colleague came to you today facing the same situation you were in, what would you tell them to do?"

*Why:* This question bypasses rationalization and surfaces genuine recommendations. It also often produces the most quotable synthesis material.

## Closing (2–3 min)

**C1 — Open floor**
"Is there anything about this topic that I haven't asked about that you think would be useful for me to understand?"

**C2 — Permission for follow-up**
"Would you be open to a quick follow-up if we have additional questions as we continue our research?"

**C3 — Referrals** *(optional)*
"Do you know anyone else who's dealt with this kind of challenge recently who might be willing to chat with us?"

---

## Interviewer reminders

**Always anchor to specific past events.** When a participant drifts into hypotheticals or general opinions, redirect: "That's helpful context. Can you think of a specific time when that happened?"

**Don't suggest answers.** Silence is more valuable than a leading question. If they struggle to answer, rephrase rather than fill in the blank for them.

**The best probes are simple:**
- "Tell me more about that."
- "What do you mean by [their word]?"
- "What happened next?"
- "How did that make you feel?"
- "Can you give me a specific example?"

**What you're listening for:**
- The hiring trigger — specific, past-tense event
- Functional job — what they were practically trying to accomplish
- Emotional job — how they wanted to feel
- Social job — how this affected their standing with others
- Progress blockers — where they got stuck
- Workarounds — what they did instead
- Switch moment — what finally made them change
